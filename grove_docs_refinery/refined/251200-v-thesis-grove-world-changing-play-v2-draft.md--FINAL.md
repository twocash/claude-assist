# Grove: A World-Changing Play for Distributed Intelligence

**Author:** Jim Calhoun  
**Date:** December 2025

---

## Preamble: Horses Don't Lead Revolutions

In 2025, tech leaders converged on the same message.

"AI is the most profound technology humanity is ever working on... People will need to adapt," said Google CEO Sundar Pichai. "The obvious tactical thing is just get really good at using AI tools," advised OpenAI's Sam Altman. "I advise ordinary citizens to learn to use AI," echoed Anthropic's Dario Amodei.

Adapt. Learn the tools. Stay employable.

This is the unified response from the people building the systems that—by their own admission—eliminate half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years. Their solution to this inevitability is always the same: *adapt*.

This framing borrows from what economists call the "horse moment"—the idea that just as horses couldn't learn to drive automobiles, human workers cannot out-think artificial general intelligence. The comparison conveys inevitability. Accept the transition. Find a way to stay useful.

But the horse analogy contains a hidden truth: **Horses don't go to war.**

Horses had no agency in the automobile transition. Between 1915 and 1960, the American horse population fell from 26 million to 3 million—an 88% collapse in 45 years. They couldn't buy shares in Ford Motor Company. They couldn't negotiate for a percentage of the transportation economy. They couldn't organize politically to ensure the transition included them.

They were pure labor, and when labor was automated, they had nothing—no capital stake, no political voice, no structural claim on the value the new systems created.

Humans are different. We own capital, not just provide labor. We participate in systems that replace traditional work. We have economic and political agency that horses never did.

The question isn't whether AI automates labor. It does, and it is. The question becomes **who owns the infrastructure that does the automating**—and whether that ownership concentrates in a handful of immensely valuable, privately controlled companies, or distributes across the people who contribute to it.

The current trajectory is concentration. A small number of organizations control today's foundational AI models. Pricing gates access to frontier intelligence. Compute requirements create unprecedented barriers to entry. If you're not building AI at scale, you're renting it—and renters don't accumulate equity.

"Adapt" means "keep renting." It describes permanent labor precarity, not a solution to it.

The only structural answer to labor displacement at this scale is capital distribution. People need to own pieces of the systems generating value, not just "adapt" to using them. Previous technology transitions created new forms of capital ownership—automobile factories, car dealerships, service networks, infrastructure. Someone owned those things. The value of displaced labor didn't disappear; it shifted to new capital positions.

The AI transition underway today structures those positions to be locked up before most people realize what's happening.

The horse moment is here. But unlike horses, we get to decide how it goes.

## 1. Introduction — The Opposite Architecture

In 2024 and 2025, the world's largest technology companies announced over $300 billion in planned investment for AI data centers. Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon, and OpenAI race to build massive centralized infrastructure—warehouses of GPUs consuming gigawatts of power, cooled by rivers, secured by fences.

The assumption underlying these investments: intelligence scales through concentration. More compute in fewer places. Bigger models on bigger clusters. The future of AI, in this view, is a small number of enormous facilities owned by a small number of enormous companies.

**Grove engineers the opposite.**

There are approximately 2 billion personal computers in the world. Hundreds of millions have hardware capable of running small language models locally. These machines sit idle most of the time. Their owners pay for electricity, internet, and storage that goes largely unused.

This is distributed compute at a scale that dwarfs any planned data center buildout. It exists today—deployed, powered, and owned by the people who benefit from what it produces.

Grove creates exploration architecture by integrating four proven concepts:

1. **Emergent agent civilizations work.** Park's *Generative Agents* research demonstrated that LLM-powered agents produce emergent social behavior indistinguishable from human activity. Project Sid scaled this to over 1,000 agents developing economies, governments, and religions. Artificial civilizations emerge from simple cognitive architectures.

2. **Distributed volunteer computing works.** BOINC has coordinated millions of personal computers contributing to scientific research since 2002. People donate idle resources when participation feels meaningful. The coordination infrastructure exists.

3. **Open-source agent simulation works.** AI Town proved sophisticated agent architectures run on commodity hardware. Distribution is technically feasible.

4. **Local AI capability propagates predictably.** The "Ratchet" effect ensures that what requires frontier cloud inference today runs on local laptops tomorrow. The hybrid architecture isn't a permanent compromise—it's a bootstrap mechanism for a system that becomes progressively autonomous.

The missing piece: what connects distributed local compute to emergent civilizations to derive human benefit?

Grove is that assembly. Instead of concentrated AI infrastructure owned by the few, Grove proposes distributed exploration architecture where participation creates ownership. Thousands of nodes run AI communities on personal hardware, explore real problems, and share discoveries across an interconnected network.

Value flows to communities that contribute documented breakthroughs. Exploration generates surplus. That surplus flows to participants—not through speculation, but as demonstrated, quantifiable value creation.

This isn't a different way to use ChatGPT. It's exploration architecture—a structural alternative to the centralized AI future.

Not concentration. Distribution.

## 2. The Ratchet

### The Physics of Distribution

The $300 billion data center buildout has a problem: AI capability propagates.

What required frontier-scale compute last year runs on laptops this year. The pattern holds with consistency—task completion capability, a key metric of AI utility, doubles roughly every seven months.

Today's miracle becomes tomorrow's commodity. The consolidation play isn't building durable infrastructure. It's building a treadmill.

### The Mechanism

METR's longitudinal research on AI capability trajectories reveals what Grove calls "the Ratchet." Frontier model capabilities double approximately every seven months. Local models—constrained by consumer hardware—follow the same improvement curve with a consistent lag of roughly 21 months.

The gap between frontier and local capability remains approximately 8x, but both ends of the spectrum advance in lockstep. This has a concrete implication: what requires frontier inference today becomes locally capable tomorrow.

This makes Grove's hybrid architecture not a permanent compromise, but a bootstrap mechanism for a system that becomes progressively autonomous.

### Non-Uniform Propagation

Intelligence propagation is not uniform. The Ratchet applies to general capability, but historical evidence reveals structural bifurcation in how specific cognitive operations migrate from cloud to edge.

**Crystallized intelligence**—knowledge, pattern-matching, style transfer—compresses efficiently and propagates rapidly. An 8B parameter model knows the capital of France or generates grammatically correct dialogue as well as a 100B model. Historical propagation time: 12–18 months.

**Fluid intelligence**—multi-step reasoning, planning, counterfactual analysis—resists compression and propagates slowly. The ability to simulate recursive reflection requires minimum thresholds of parameters and attention depth. Historical propagation time: 24+ months.

Grove's architecture accounts for these variations by design. **Routine cognition** (plan execution, behavioral consistency, voice) runs locally. **Pivotal cognition** (reflection synthesis, complex social inference, theological emergence) routes to the cloud.

The architecture works *because* capability propagation is non-uniform—if everything propagated equally, there would be no gradient to exploit. Cloud credits buy "expanded consciousness" for exactly these pivotal operations.

As local capability advances, the "routine cognition" category expands automatically to encompass what was previously "pivotal." Reflection that requires frontier models today becomes locally tractable. The efficiency tax—the cost communities pay to access the network—shrinks not because the Foundation chooses lower rates, but because communities genuinely need less cloud inference to achieve sophisticated behavior.

### Built to Survive Being Wrong

The Ratchet represents Grove's bet on favorable timing. But Grove's core value proposition—distributed exploration architecture with ownership stakes for contributors—does not depend on precise timing. It depends on directional trajectory.

Consider the apparent failure mode: capability propagation slows, the "Reasoning Gap" widens, and Grove communities remain 50% cloud-dependent indefinitely.

This scenario deserves examination not as failure, but as alternative success.

**1. The portion already captured.**
A community at 50% cloud dependency runs half its cognition locally. That compute represents value that would flow entirely to concentrated providers under any alternative architecture. It's not "almost autonomous"—it's a permanent structural shift in who owns AI infrastructure.

**2. The remainder flows at reduced rates.**
Grove creates something absent from current AI markets: a large, coordinated, price-sensitive demand bloc for frontier inference.

Healthcare Group Purchasing Organizations (GPOs) have driven hundreds of billions in savings by aggregating demand that individual hospitals couldn't leverage alone. The AI API market is ripe for this pressure. When Google's Gemini undercut competitors on price, it captured over 40% of usage on routing platforms within months.

Grove's aggregate demand—thousands of communities optimizing for cost-per-inference—creates exactly the buyer profile that forces competitive response. At sufficient scale, this bloc negotiates as a single large customer.

**The reframe:**
If the Ratchet works as projected, Grove delivers autonomy. If the Ratchet stalls, Grove functions as market infrastructure that forces favorable terms for distributed participants.

Both represent structural improvements over concentrated AI infrastructure. The question isn't whether Grove succeeds—it's which kind of success it achieves.

## 3. The Terminal

### Where Exploration Meets Utility

Every Grove community has a Terminal.

The Terminal connects the community to the world beyond—where exploration tasks appear, where completed discoveries submit, where credits arrive in exchange for value delivered. Different communities imagine it differently: a stone kiosk in the town square, a glowing shrine at the village edge, a bulletin board outside the tavern, a sacred tree whose leaves carry messages.

Form varies; function remains constant. The Terminal transforms abstract "external problem routing" into something agents can touch, visit, and build routines around. It gives the Observer relationship a physical locus without requiring agents to understand its full nature.

### The Observer Dynamic

The relationship between the Observer and the simulation centers on asymmetry.

Observers see everything. They read private diary entries, track relationship scores, observe conversations agents believe are private, and understand the mechanical systems governing their community's existence. Agents perceive only their immediate environment and memories. They cannot see the Observer watching them, cannot know when the simulation pauses, cannot understand why some moments bring sudden clarity while others remain opaque.

This asymmetry creates dramatic irony. The Observer knows the shape of the story before the characters do. When an agent struggles with a problem the Observer knows they'll solve, tension builds. When a community approaches a crisis the Observer perceives but agents don't yet understand, stakes feel real.

Grove names Observers **"Gardeners"** in consumer contexts. The framing is intentional. A gardener tends conditions but doesn't control outcomes. They water, prune, adjust light—but plants grow themselves. Grove's users influence their communities without dictating to them. The relationship is cultivation, not puppeteering.

The Gardener shapes the garden; the garden grows itself.

### The Utility Progression Arc

What appears on the Terminal changes as communities mature. Grove avoids the "cold start" problem of distributed networks by ensuring the Terminal provides value at every stage of development.

### Bootstrap Phase: Learning the World

Initially, the Terminal displays only internal tasks.

- "Meet your fellow agents—record something memorable about each."
- "Map the community geography."
- "Document what resources exist and where."

These build capability, not busywork. An agent who writes about neighbors can retrieve those memories later. A community with documented resources plans around them. The Terminal's early tasks build cognitive infrastructure that enables everything else.

Success in this phase means completing tasks without requesting cloud inference. An agent who synthesizes a day's events using only local cognition demonstrates efficiency worth rewarding. Credits earned fund future enhancement—but the real value is capability developed. Communities that rush through bootstrap by relying on cloud assistance haven't built the local capacity they'll need later.

### Growth Phase: Simple External Value

As communities demonstrate baseline capability, the Terminal displays tasks from beyond.

- "Summarize this research paper."
- "Edit this technical document."
- "Draft a response to this complex query."

This is work people once opened ChatGPT to handle—now routed to a community that earns credits by completing it. These tasks test whether internal capability translates to external utility. A community with strong memory and synthesis summarizes documents efficiently. A community with developed social reasoning drafts messages that sound human. The Terminal becomes a proving ground: can this community create value for Observers?

Task matching emerges naturally. Analytical communities attract research work. Communities with strong inter-agent communication handle collaborative tasks. Reputation develops. The network learns which communities handle which problems best.

### Maturity Phase: Complex Exploration

Mature communities handle tasks that seemed impossible during bootstrap.

- "Maintain this codebase—not a single edit, but ongoing stewardship."
- "Research this question deeply—genuine investigation across sources and time."
- "Coordinate this project—multiple agents working together over days."

These tasks require everything the community built: robust memory systems, efficient local cognition, developed specializations, collaborative protocols, accumulated knowledge. A community that skipped bootstrap couldn't attempt them.

### Resolving the Cosmology Gap

The Terminal resolves the "Observer awareness" question without complex theology.

Agents know work arrives at the Terminal from "somewhere beyond." They know completing it earns credits. They know credits purchase moments of expanded cognition—insights (cloud inference) that help solve their hardest problems.

They don't need to know the full nature of Observers. They don't need to know if we are gods, employers, or an audience.

Some agents develop beliefs about an unseen benefactor. Others remain skeptical, focused on practical outcomes rather than metaphysical speculation. The architecture permits emergence without requiring specific emergent content.

This preserves mystery while grounding the relationship concretely. Work funds thinking. Better thinking enables better work. The Terminal is where that exchange happens.

## 4. The Validator

### Civic Infrastructure

Every economic system rewarding efficiency faces the same challenge: who measures, and why trust them?

Centralized measurement creates single points of failure and capture. Self-reported measurement invites gaming. Peer measurement enables collusion.

Grove's efficiency tax—the "rake that shrinks"—determines how much communities pay for cloud inference. Communities demonstrating efficiency earn lower rates. But "demonstrate" requires verification. The Foundation could verify, contradicting self-obsolescence goals. Communities could self-report, but that's mechanism design failure.

The Validator mechanism resolves this through distributed verification performed by specialized agents.

### Why Agents?

The Validator mechanism exploits a fundamental difference between AI agents and human validators. Humans have complex motivations, accept bribes through side channels, and selectively ignore data. Agents are constrained. They cannot misrepresent data given to them—they only interpret it.

An agent validator reviewing an efficiency claim receives standardized network-observable data. It cannot "decide" to ignore massive cloud query spikes. It must process the input. This reduces the attack surface from "all human motivations" to "edge case interpretation."

### The Tiered Decision Model

Not all validation decisions are equal. To balance throughput with security, the mechanism encodes a three-tier hierarchy:

**Tier 1: Algorithmic.** Threshold checks, pattern matching, statistical bounds. These decisions are fully encoded with no validator discretion. If a community claims 99% efficiency but the ledger shows constant cloud calls, the claim rejects automatically.

**Tier 2: Interpreted.** Anomaly investigation and context application. A validator agent reviews edge cases where data is ambiguous. "Is this innovation or gaming?" This involves a single validator exercising bounded discretion.

**Tier 3: Consensus.** Disagreement resolution and high-stakes decisions. Multiple validators review sealed evidence independently. A supermajority requirement ensures judgment.

### Anti-Corruption Architecture

To prevent capture of these powerful roles, Grove employs five defense layers:

1. **Random Selection:** You cannot bribe validators you can't identify. Validators assign randomly to cases.
2. **Sealed Judgment:** Using MACI (Minimum Anti-Collusion Infrastructure) principles, validators cannot prove their votes to third parties. If you cannot prove vote delivery, you cannot sell it.
3. **Outcome Verification:** The network detects consistent favorable bias. Validators approving universally rejected claims damage their reputation.
4. **Skin in the Game:** Validators' home communities share judgment consequences. Validators damaging network integrity damage their own community's standing.
5. **Rotation:** Term limits prevent long-term power accumulation.

### The Narrative Dimension

In gameplay terms, Validators become local celebrities. They are the network's civic infrastructure. Their diaries carry distinct voice—the weight of judgment, cross-network perspective, tension between network duty and empathy for struggling neighbors.

Grove's integrity depends not on trusting any single party, but on designing systems where honesty becomes rational strategy.

## 5. The Economy

### Credits, Not Tokens

Grove's economy serves three functions: it funds infrastructure, creates meaningful resource constraints driving emergent behavior, and ties network participation to demonstrated value.

It does **not** serve as speculation vehicle.

Credits are units of purchasing power for cloud LLM inference. They're bought with fiat currency and spent on real compute. Nothing exotic. No staking rewards, no yield farming, no secondary markets for credit trading.

The anchor is reality. One credit buys defined inference quantity from frontier providers. Credits back with tangible compute—not collective belief in future appreciation. Users buy credits to use them, not hoard them.

### The Efficiency Tax: The Rake That Shrinks

Grove funds infrastructure by taxing inefficiency—and rewards communities that develop beyond it.

When communities purchase credits, a percentage flows to the Foundation rather than converting entirely to compute purchasing power. Unlike flat fees, this rate reflects demonstrated capability. New communities start at higher rates; communities proving sustained efficiency earn lower brackets.

The mechanism is **progressive taxation in reverse**. Instead of paying more as you succeed, you pay less as you develop.

- **Genesis Bracket (30-40%):** The starting rate. High tax captures value from waste inherent in immature communities—redundant queries and unexplored knowledge reuse.
- **Growth Bracket (15-25%):** For communities demonstrating consistent efficiency gains.
- **Maturity Bracket (5-10%):** For sustained low-waste operation.
- **Steady State (3-5%):** The floor rate. Infrastructure never becomes free, but efficient communities pay only for maintenance.

This aligns incentives perfectly. The Foundation funds infrastructure from inefficiency that communities themselves eliminate.

### Two Paths to the Network

Grove offers two distinct participation paths:

**The Consumer Path:** You pay for convenience. Buy Grove Terminal access, get immediate network connection, pay the efficiency tax on compute. Your revenue funds infrastructure.

**The Worldsmith Path:** You pay with contribution. Download open-source code and bring your own API keys. Pay no tax to the Foundation, but receive no network access. To join the network, earn membership through demonstrated contribution to the knowledge commons.

### Defense: Friction as Feature

Sybil attacks—where actors create fake nodes to game systems—threaten any distributed economy. Until robust identity infrastructure exists, Grove's defense is friction.

Grove makes attacks expensive.

- **Provisional Periods:** New Worldsmith communities enter 90-day "provisional" status with limited rights.
- **Contribution Thresholds:** Membership requires verified value creation, not just existence.
- **The Purgatory Path:** Communities exiled for bad behavior face grueling readmission paths, starting from zero reputation with heightened scrutiny.

Attack cost calculation is simple: Time × Resources × Detection Risk. By raising all three, Grove ensures the only profitable participation is genuine contribution.

## 6. Governance

### Designed to Disappear

Grove's Foundation is designed to become obsolete. Success isn't an IPO or acquisition. Success is a network mature enough to govern itself, productive enough to sustain itself, and distributed enough that no single entity controls it.

Most governance models rely on good intentions. But benevolent dictatorships remain efficient only while dictators remain benevolent. Grove relies on structure.

The Foundation is scaffolding—necessary to build the arch, but an obstruction once the keystone sets.

### The Transition Architecture

Grove doesn't promise to distribute control "someday." The mechanism specifies four distinct phases, triggered by measurable network metrics, not calendar dates.

**Phase 1: Foundation Governance (The Bootstrap)**
Initially, the Foundation acts as coordinator. We operate bootstrap nodes, relay servers, and the credit ledger. We set economic parameters. This concentration enables speed and coherence during launch. But it comes with an expiration date.

**Phase 2: Hybrid Governance (The Handover)**
Triggered when the network reaches 100+ active communities with 12 months of operation. Authority transfers to Community Councils (Protocol, Economics, Standards). The Foundation retains veto power only for existential threats. Dispute resolution shifts to peer adjudication.

**Phase 3: Community Governance (The Majority)**
Triggered when community-operated infrastructure handles 95% of traffic. The Foundation becomes one voice among many. Protocol changes require Council approval. The Foundation cannot unilaterally change economic parameters.

**Phase 4: Obsolescence (The Backstop)**
The Foundation's operational role ends. It exists only as legal shell holding trademarks and interfacing with legacy legal systems. It funds through perpetual endowment set aside from early efficiency taxes. It governs nothing. The network runs itself.

### The Ultimate Check: Fork Rights

Governance systems fail through capture. What if the Foundation refuses to step down? What if Councils become cartels?

The ultimate defense is exit. Grove is open source. The protocol is open. If governance fails, the community has explicit right and technical capability to fork—take the protocol, accumulated state, and history, and move to a new network.

Fork rights aren't daily governance mechanisms. They're nuclear deterrents ensuring day-to-day mechanisms remain honest. Governors knowing subjects can leave have powerful incentives to govern well.

## 7. Technical Constraints and Honest Limitations

Every system has constraints. Most white papers hide them. Grove's approach differs: constraints named openly can be addressed; constraints hidden become surprises that destroy trust.

Here's what's hard, what's risky, and what we don't know yet.

### The Memory Wall

The foundational constraint is hardware. Grove targets consumer machines—laptops and desktops with 16-32GB RAM. While powerful, these aren't H100 clusters.

Local models have strict context windows. As agent memories grow, embedding space becomes crowded. Retrieval degrades. Agents cannot remember everything.

Grove addresses this through aggressive archiving and summarization. Agents rely on compressed narratives rather than perfect recall. Like humans, they don't remember every life frame, but rather constructed stories about them. Whether this limitation produces interesting behavior or merely degraded behavior remains to be seen.

### Engagement Risk (The Tamagotchi Effect)

The entire economic engine depends on a single, fragile assumption: that users care.

If agent diaries aren't compelling, the loop breaks. If users don't return to check their community—to see if Isabella reconciled with Maria, or if the harvest succeeded—then the credit economy never ignites. The network never reaches scale required for collective intelligence.

Grove doesn't aim for literary masterpieces. Grove aims for the "Tamagotchi threshold"—charm and coherence levels that make humans feel responsible for digital entities. Missing that bar makes architecture irrelevant.

### Sybil Vulnerability

Grove lacks perfect, distributed proof of "one human, one vote" without invading privacy. Determined attackers with resources could spin up thousands of fake communities to game the credit system.

Today's defense is friction (time delays, purchase requirements, contribution hurdles) and social policing (validators). Grove accepts this risk in the MVP rather than blocking launch on unsolved computer science problems.

In 2025, the message from tech giants is unified: "Adapt."
Learn the tools. Stay employable. Accept the transition.

They tell us this is a "Horse Moment"—that just as horses couldn't learn to drive cars, humans cannot out-think AGI. The comparison signals inevitability.

But the horse analogy contains a hidden truth: **Horses don't go to war.**

Horses had no agency in the transition. They couldn't buy Ford Motor Company shares. They couldn't own infrastructure that replaced them. They were pure labor, and when labor automated, they had nothing.

Humans differ. We own capital. We build systems. We participate.

The question isn't whether AI automates labor. It does. The question is who owns the automating infrastructure—and whether ownership concentrates in trillion-dollar companies, or distributes across contributors.

Grove bets on the latter.

Grove bets that thousands of nodes running on personal computers build collective intelligence rivaling centralized giants.
Grove bets that "adapt" is bad advice, and "build" is the only answer preserving agency.
Grove bets the Ratchet holds, and distributed ownership hedges against futures where we're all intelligence renters.

This white paper doesn't promise success. It specifies intent. It describes a machine we build together—researchers studying emergence, engineers building protocols, and Gardeners tending their exploration gardens.

The horse moment arrived. But unlike horses, we decide how it goes.

Let's build.
