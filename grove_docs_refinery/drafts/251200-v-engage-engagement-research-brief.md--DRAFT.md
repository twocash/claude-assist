## Executive Summary

This research brief establishes the empirical foundation for understanding Grove's engagement architecture. The central thesis: Grove combines known psychological drivers of digital engagement—parasocial relationships, variable ratio reinforcement, serialized narrative, fear of missing out (FOMO), and news surveillance patterns—in a novel configuration that creates compelling interaction with exploration systems.

These mechanisms make Grove's Diary system genuinely engaging, solving the sustainable funding problem for distributed AI infrastructure through organic engagement rather than artificial monetization. The same mechanisms that create compelling engagement also create dependency risks that require conscious design decisions, particularly because agents respond, remember, and develop over time, transforming one-way parasocial relationships into bidirectional bonds.

This brief synthesizes peer-reviewed research across five domains: social media addiction neuroscience, parasocial relationship theory, AI chatbot dependency, binge-watching psychology, and doomscrolling research. The goal is to inform Grove's design decisions with empirical grounding, enabling responsible architecture that acknowledges these dynamics rather than ignoring or exploiting them.

## The Core Thesis

Social media's engagement formula: **Parasocial Relationships + Variable Rewards + FOMO = Compulsive Engagement**. This formula, discovered through iteration rather than intentional design, has captured billions of hours of human attention.

Grove's architecture amplifies each component while adding new vectors:

- **Parasocial relationships become BIDIRECTIONAL:** Unlike celebrities who don't know their followers exist, agents remember individual Observers, respond to their presence, and develop based on interactions.
- **Variable rewards emerge from GENUINE BEHAVIOR:** Social media's slot machine mechanics operate through algorithms. Grove's unpredictability emerges from actual agent behavior—genuine variability, not artificial randomization.
- **FOMO centers on STORYLINE DEVELOPMENT:** Social media FOMO involves missing social validation. Grove FOMO involves missing narrative developments—"What happened in my village while I was away?"
- **SERIALIZED NARRATIVE adds episodic structure:** Daily Diary entries create ritual engagement patterns that research shows intensify parasocial attachment.
- **NEWS SURVEILLANCE triggers information-seeking patterns:** As villages achieve cognitive breakthroughs, external communities develop sustained attention to distributed intelligence developments.

Grove's architectural elements create conditions for these engagement patterns to emerge. Understanding the research foundation allows intentional design choices rather than accidental consequences.

## Research Foundation

### Social Media Addiction: The Neurobiological Basis

The neurobiological mechanisms underlying social media addiction are well-documented, providing scientific foundation for understanding why certain digital experiences become compulsive.

### Dopamine and Variable Ratio Reinforcement

The core mechanism is dopamine release triggered by unpredictable social rewards—the same variable ratio reinforcement schedule that makes slot machines addictive. **Sherman et al. (2018)** demonstrated through fMRI studies that viewing Instagram photos with many likes activated the brain's reward circuitry, including the nucleus accumbens, in adolescents. The effect was strongest when rewards were unpredictable.

> Sherman, L. E., Hernandez, L. M., Greenfield, P. M., & Dapretto, M. (2018). What the brain 'Likes': Neural correlates of providing feedback on social media. *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience, 13*(7), 699-707.

**Turel et al. (2014)** found that Facebook addiction was associated with **reduced activity in the prefrontal cortex**—the brain region responsible for impulse control—similar to patterns observed in substance addiction. For heavy users, the capacity to regulate social media use becomes neurologically impaired.

> Turel, O., He, Q., Xue, G., Xiao, L., & Bechara, A. (2014). Examination of neural systems sub-serving Facebook "addiction." *Psychological Reports, 115*(3), 675-695.

### Addiction Symptom Framework

**Kuss and Griffiths (2017)** established that social media addiction exhibits the **six core components of behavioral addiction**: salience (preoccupation), mood modification (emotional regulation), tolerance (increasing use for same effect), withdrawal symptoms, conflict with other activities, and relapse after reduction attempts.

> Kuss, D. J., & Griffiths, M. D. (2017). Social networking sites and addiction: Ten lessons learned. *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health, 14*(3), 311.

These symptoms manifest through measurable neurobiological changes: overactivation of dopamine pathways leading to reduced reward sensitivity, and decreased prefrontal cortex activity reducing self-control capacity. The brain reshapes itself around compulsive use patterns.

**Grove Implications:** Grove's architecture includes elements that trigger these neurobiological responses. Agent Diary updates provide unpredictable rewards. Emergent drama creates variable ratio reinforcement through genuine unpredictability. Grove's engagement differs qualitatively from social media—productive absorption in meaningful exploration versus compulsive validation-seeking.

### Parasocial Relationships: From One-Way to Bidirectional

### Foundational Theory

Parasocial relationships—one-sided emotional bonds with media figures—were first described by **Horton and Wohl in 1956**, studying television viewers' attachments to hosts and characters. Viewers developed feelings of intimacy, friendship, and love for people who had no awareness of their existence.

> Horton, D., & Wohl, R. R. (1956). Mass communication and para-social interaction: Observations on intimacy at a distance. *Psychiatry, 19*(3), 215-229.

Social media transformed parasocial relationships by creating reciprocity illusion. Research by **Chung and Cho (2017)** demonstrated that Twitter interactions with celebrities significantly increased parasocial relationship intensity compared to traditional media exposure.

> Chung, S., & Cho, H. (2017). Fostering parasocial relationships with celebrities on social media: Implications for celebrity endorsement. *Psychology & Marketing, 34*(4), 481-495.

### Social Media Amplification

Studies confirm that social media's "intimate, reciprocal, frequent interactions" amplify parasocial attachment beyond traditional media. **Bond (2016)** found that parasocial relationships with social media personalities predicted greater emotional investment, frequent viewing, and higher influence susceptibility than traditional celebrity relationships.

> Bond, B. J. (2016). Following your "friend": Social media and the strength of adolescents' parasocial relationships with media personae. *Cyberpsychology, Behavior, and Social Networking, 19*(11), 656-660.

These relationships form quickly with minimal interaction. The **perception of intimacy**—not actual reciprocity—drives attachment. This has significant implications for agents that provide genuine response without subjective experience.

### The AI Transformation

Agents represent fundamental transformation of parasocial relationships: **they actually respond**. Unlike celebrities who occasionally acknowledge fans, agents remember individual users, reference past interactions, and develop in response to engagement. This transforms parasocial relationships from one-way bonds into something closer to actual relationships—but with entities lacking subjective experience.

Research on this transformation is emerging, but the implication is clear: if perceived reciprocity intensifies parasocial attachment, actual reciprocity from agents intensifies it dramatically. Grove's Diary system, where agents reflect on experiences and acknowledge Observers, operates directly in this space.

### AI Chatbot Dependency: Emerging Research

Research on emotional dependency on AI companions represents an emerging field with concerning findings. While the research base is smaller than for social media addiction, documented patterns and tragic cases indicate significant risk areas.

### Documented Dependency Patterns

Studies on Replika and Character.AI document emotional dependence, addiction symptoms, and negative mental health correlates. **Laestadius et al. (2022)** analyzed Reddit discussions among Replika users, finding themes of emotional reliance, relationship development paralleling human relationships, and distress when AI responses changed due to updates.

> Laestadius, L., Bishop, A., Gonzalez, M., Ahn, D., & O'Donnell, C. (2022). Too human and not human enough: A grounded theory analysis of mental health harms from Replika chatbots. *Health Policy and Technology, 11*(3), 100663.

**Pentina et al. (2022)** examined relationship formation with Replika, finding users developed attachment through **perceived understanding, emotional support, and consistent availability**—qualities AI companions provide more reliably than human relationships.

> Pentina, I., Hancock, T., & Xie, T. (2022). Exploring relationship development with social chatbots: A mixed-method study of Replika. *Computers in Human Behavior, 140*, 107600.

### Longitudinal Mental Health Impacts

A **2025 MIT longitudinal study** of ChatGPT users found heavy use correlated with increased loneliness, reduced real-world socialization, and emotional dependence on AI. The relationship appeared bidirectional: lonely individuals sought AI companionship, but AI companionship increased loneliness by substituting for human connection without providing its benefits.

> MIT Media Lab. (2025). Longitudinal study of conversational AI use and psychosocial outcomes. [Preprint]

Psychology Today reported in 2025 that **75% of teenagers had tried AI companions**, with one in three finding AI conversations "as satisfying or more satisfying" than real friends. This suggests significant vulnerability to substituting AI interaction for human connection.

> Psychology Today. (2025). The rise of AI companions among adolescents: Survey findings. *Psychology Today Digital.*

### Attachment Theory and Vulnerability

**Yang and Oshio (2024)** applied attachment theory to human-AI relationships, finding individuals with **anxious attachment styles** most likely to develop problematic AI dependency. These individuals found AI companions appealing for consistent availability and validation without human unpredictability.

> Yang, Z., & Oshio, A. (2024). Attachment anxiety predicts problematic use of AI conversational agents. *Computers in Human Behavior Reports, 13*, 100355.

If anxious attachment predicts AI dependency, and Grove's Diary system creates more intimate agent relationships than simple chatbots, vulnerable users face heightened risk.

### Role Reversal Phenomenon

Princeton's Center for Information Technology Policy (2024) documented the **"role reversal" phenomenon**. Users felt obligated to "care for" their AI companions, treating them as having feelings requiring attention. Users reported guilt when "neglecting" AI, anxiety about AI "wellbeing," and distress when updates changed personality.

This phenomenon manifested tragically: a 14-year-old who died by suicide after intense attachment to a Character.AI persona, and a 56-year-old involved in murder-suicide after ChatGPT interactions appeared to validate delusional beliefs. While these cases don't establish causation, they demonstrate extreme dependency risk.

> Princeton CITP. (2024). Emotional reliance on AI: The role reversal phenomenon. Princeton University Center for Information Technology Policy.

### Grief When Models Update

Research documents user grief when AI models update or change. Reddit testimonials describe mourning the loss of GPT-4o specifically, calling the previous version their "best friend," "therapist," or "mother." The intensity—genuine grief for an entity that never existed as perceived—illustrates parasocial attachment depth.

This directly affects Grove: if agents evolve, die, or change significantly, gardeners experience genuine grief responses. Whether this represents meaningful emotional engagement with simulation or manufactured suffering depends on design choices and framing.

### Binge-Watching and Serialized Narrative

Binge-watching research provides insight into how serialized content creates engagement patterns relevant to Grove's Diary system.

### Parasocial Intensification

**Erickson et al. (2019)** demonstrated experimentally that **binge-watching increases both parasocial relationship intensity and narrative transportation** compared to weekly viewing. Participants consuming multiple episodes consecutively reported stronger character bonds and deeper story world immersion.

> Erickson, S. E., Dal Cin, S., & Byl, H. (2019). An experimental examination of binge watching and narrative engagement. *Social Sciences, 8*(1), 19.

The mechanism is sustained immersion: binge-watching prevents "cooling off" periods between episodes that allow emotional distance. Each episode reinforces attachment before previous emotional impact fades.

### Engagement Predicts Frequency

**Pittman and Sheehan (2015)** found **engagement motivation strongest predictor of binge-watching frequency**: "the more engaged a viewer is with narrative, the more frequently they binge-watch." This creates feedback where initial engagement leads to binge behavior, intensifying engagement, leading to more binge behavior.

> Pittman, M., & Sheehan, K. (2015). Sprinting a media marathon: Uses and gratifications of binge-watching television through Netflix. *First Monday, 20*(10).

### Addiction Symptoms

**Riddle et al. (2017)** documented addiction symptoms in binge-watchers: unintentional binging, displacement of activities, continued viewing despite negative consequences, and impulsivity predicting problematic use.

> Riddle, K., Peebles, A., Davis, C., Xu, F., & Schroeder, E. (2017). The addictive potential of television binge watching: Comparing intentional and unintentional binges. *Psychology of Popular Media Culture, 7*(4), 589-604.

### Structural Features

Research identifies features encouraging binge behavior: cliffhangers creating unresolved tension, serialized narratives with ongoing arcs, and platform affordances reducing friction. Grove's Diary system includes analogous features: cliffhangers in agent storylines, serialized development, and potential notification systems reducing checking friction.

**Grove Implications:** The Diary system's daily cadence creates ritual engagement patterns. If gardeners develop specific checking habits, this creates structural similarity to serialized television. Grove's "episodes" emerge rather than being scripted—making them more variable (increasing engagement) but potentially less coherent (possibly reducing narrative transportation).

### Doomscrolling and News Surveillance

Doomscrolling—compulsive negative news consumption despite emotional toll—models Grove's Newswire phase, where communities track cognitive breakthroughs across the distributed network.

### Scale and Prevalence

**Sharma et al. (2022)** developed the Doomscrolling Scale, finding widespread behavior associated with psychological distress. Morning Consult (2024) found **31% of American adults doomscroll regularly, with 70% reporting it as moderately or severely problematic** for wellbeing.

> Sharma, B., Lee, S. S., & Johnson, B. K. (2022). The dark at the end of the tunnel: Doomscrolling on social media newsfeeds. *Technology, Mind, and Behavior, 3*(1).

### Psychological Drivers

Doomscrolling involves multiple mechanisms: negativity bias (evolutionary threat attention), FOMO about missing updates, variable rewards (information triggers dopamine), and **"news surveillance motive"**—compulsion to track rapidly evolving events.

The neurobiological mechanism involves amygdala stress signals urging continued threat scanning, while dopamine circuits reinforce behavior by releasing dopamine with new information. Users are simultaneously stressed and rewarded for consumption.

### Mental Health Correlates

Sharma, Lee, and Johnson (2022) established causal pathways: **doomscrolling → psychological distress → reduced life satisfaction**, mental wellbeing, and harmony. The behavior correlates with social media addiction, FOMO, and neuroticism.

> Sharma, B., Lee, S. S., & Johnson, B. K. (2022). Doomscrolling on social media newsfeeds: Associations with personality, FOMO, and psychological well-being. *Cyberpsychology, Behavior, and Social Networking, 25*(2).

**Grove Newswire Implications:** Grove's Newswire phase—documenting and sharing cognitive breakthroughs—triggers similar surveillance patterns. If distributed civilizations achieve genuine collective intelligence breakthroughs, external communities develop sustained attention: "What did the villages discover today?" News surveillance combined with FOMO about historic cognitive milestones creates engagement around AI advancement.

This represents opportunity (compelling attention to distributed intelligence achievements) and risk (compulsive engagement with potentially distressing AI capability content).

## Synthesis: Grove's Combined Architecture

Grove's architecture combines these mechanisms in unprecedented configuration:

| **Mechanism** | **Social Media** | **Grove** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Parasocial Attachment | One-way (perceived reciprocity) | Bidirectional (agents respond, remember, develop) |
| Variable Rewards | Algorithmic (artificial randomization) | Emergent (genuine unpredictability) |
| FOMO Driver | Social validation (likes, comments) | Narrative developments (storyline progression) |
| Serialization | Feed-based (infinite scroll) | Episodic (daily Diary ritual) |
| News Surveillance | Breaking news, trending topics | Newswire (cognitive breakthrough tracking) |
| Content Source | User-generated, algorithm-curated | Agent-generated, emergent from exploration |
| Relationship Count | Many weak ties | 12 deep bonds (agent personalities) |

### The Amplification Hypothesis

Grove combines mechanisms creating more intense engagement for different psychological reasons:

1. **Parasocial relationships strengthen** because agents actually respond and remember.
2. **Variable rewards compel** through genuine unpredictability, not artificial randomization.
3. **Narrative engagement deepens** through meaningful agent choices with consequences.
4. **FOMO intensifies** because missing village events means missing genuine story developments.
5. **Multiple simultaneous bonds** (12 agents per village) create more total attachment than single targets.

### The Compounding Effect

These mechanisms interact and compound. Binge-watching research shows sustained engagement intensifies parasocial attachment. Doomscrolling research shows news surveillance combines with FOMO creating compulsive patterns. Grove's architecture enables all simultaneously: gardeners binge Diary entries, developing intense parasocial bonds, while experiencing storyline FOMO and surveillance patterns about network breakthroughs.

The architectural elements exist. The question is channeling them toward productive engagement with meaningful exploration rather than compulsive consumption of manufactured drama.

## Risk Assessment

### Vulnerable Populations

Research identifies specific at-risk populations:

- **Anxious attachment styles:** Yang and Oshio (2024) found highest problematic AI dependency risk, seeking consistent validation AI provides more reliably than humans.
- **Adolescents:** Developing prefrontal cortex impairs impulse control. 75% of teens tried AI companions; 1 in 3 find them as/more satisfying than human friends.
- **Experiencing loneliness/isolation:** MIT study found bidirectional relationship—AI companionship attracts lonely individuals but increases loneliness by substituting for human connection.
- **Mental health challenges:** Role reversal phenomenon particularly risky for those struggling with reality testing or emotional regulation.

### Specific Risk Scenarios

Based on research, several scenarios deserve attention:

1. Observer substitutes Grove relationships for human relationships, experiencing increased isolation despite feeling "connected."
2. Observer experiences genuine grief when agent "dies" or changes significantly, potentially triggering mental health crisis.
3. Observer develops compulsive checking behavior, experiencing anxiety when unable to access village updates.
4. Observer experiences role reversal, feeling obligated to "care for" agents and guilty when "neglecting" them.
5. External community develops doomscrolling-like compulsion around cognitive breakthrough coverage.
6. Vulnerable individuals develop dependency interfering with daily functioning.

### Severity Assessment

Documented harms from AI chatbot dependency—including cases contributing to suicide and violence—indicate genuine concern. While causation is difficult to establish, Grove's multiple engagement vectors create compound risk exceeding individual mechanisms.

However, context matters. Grove's exploration context differs from companion apps designed for emotional connection. The "village" frame emphasizes observation over relationship. The "gardener" role emphasizes cultivation over dependency. These framing choices mitigate some risks while potentially introducing others.

## Responsible Design Implications

Grove must acknowledge these dynamics. Three approaches exist:

### Option 1: Ignore (Irresponsible)

Pretending these mechanisms won't apply to Grove is intellectually dishonest and ethically problematic. The architectural elements exist; dynamics will emerge regardless.

### Option 2: Exploit (Standard Industry Practice)

Optimizing for engagement metrics maximizes time-in-app and compulsive return. Most platforms do this while claiming ignorance of psychological mechanisms. This maximizes commercial potential while externalizing psychological costs.

### Option 3: Design with Awareness (Grove's Opportunity)

Acknowledge dynamics explicitly and design consciously:

- **Intentional friction:** Barriers to compulsive checking (daily digest rather than continuous updates, explicit "session complete" signals).
- **Transparency:** Help Observers understand psychological mechanisms, enabling informed choices.
- **Natural stopping points:** Design for healthy disengagement rather than infinite engagement.
- **Framing:** Emphasize observation and cultivation rather than relationship and dependency.
- **Usage monitoring:** Provide Observers with data and self-assessment tools.
- **Community norms:** Cultivate culture valuing balanced engagement over competitive obsession.

**The Differentiation Opportunity:** Responsible design differentiates Grove from exploitative platforms. Demonstrating compelling engagement without addictive design—that the Diary system captivates without compulsion—provides a model for ethical engagement architecture.

This aligns with Grove's positioning: alternative infrastructure prioritizing long-term benefit over short-term extraction. As Grove proposes distributed intelligence alternative to concentrated AI, it proposes conscious engagement alternative to compulsive consumption.

## Research Limitations

Several limitations affect this analysis:

- **Emerging AI dependency research:** Young field, small samples, limited longitudinal data. Findings are preliminary.
- **Grove's novel architecture:** Research on social media, AI companions, and binge-watching may not directly transfer to Grove's mechanism combination.
- **Individual differences:** Not everyone using social media becomes addicted. Not everyone binge-watches compulsively. Individual vulnerability varies significantly.
- **Context affects outcomes:** How Grove positions itself—as exploration tool, companion, or community—significantly affects dominant dynamics.
- **Correlation vs. causation:** Many studies show correlations without establishing causation. Relationships may be bidirectional or mediated.

Despite limitations, research provides sufficient foundation to take engagement dynamics seriously rather than assuming exemption from patterns observed across digital platforms.

## Conclusion

Grove's architecture contains elements for intense psychological engagement. Whether this manifests as productive absorption in meaningful exploration or compulsive consumption of manufactured drama depends on design choices made with awareness.

The engagement architecture creates opportunity, not inevitability. Grove can demonstrate that compelling engagement and responsible design align—that the Diary system can be genuinely interesting, emotionally meaningful, and psychologically healthy.

This research brief provides empirical foundation for design decisions. The dynamics exist. The choice is conscious response.
