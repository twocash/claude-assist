# The Grove: A World-Changing Play for Exploration Architecture

**Author:** Jim Calhoun  
**Date:** December 2025

---

## Preamble: Horses Don't Lead Revolutions

In 2025, tech leaders converged on the same message.

"AI is the most profound technology humanity is ever working on... People will need to adapt," said Google CEO Sundar Pichai. "The obvious tactical thing is just get really good at using AI tools," advised OpenAI's Sam Altman. "I advise ordinary citizens to learn to use AI," echoed Anthropic's Dario Amodei.

Adapt. Learn the tools. Stay employable.

This is the unified response from the people building systems that—by their own admission—will eliminate half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years. Their solution to this inevitability: *adapt*.

This framing borrows from economists' "horse moment"—the idea that just as horses couldn't learn to drive automobiles, human workers cannot out-think artificial general intelligence. The comparison conveys inevitability. Accept the transition. Find a way to stay useful.

But the horse analogy contains a hidden truth: **Horses don't go to war.**

Horses had no agency in the automobile transition. Between 1915 and 1960, the American horse population fell from 26 million to 3 million—an 88% collapse. They couldn't buy shares in Ford Motor Company. They couldn't negotiate for transportation economy percentages. They couldn't organize politically.

They were pure labor. When labor was automated, they had nothing—no capital stake, no political voice, no structural claim on the new systems' value.

Humans are different. We own capital, not just provide labor. We participate in systems that replace traditional work. We have economic and political agency horses never did.

The question isn't whether AI automates labor—it already is. The question becomes **who owns the infrastructure**—concentrated in privately controlled companies, or distributed across contributors.

The current trajectory is concentration. A small number of organizations control foundational AI models. Pricing gates access. Compute requirements create unprecedented barriers. If you're not building AI at scale, you're renting—and renters don't accumulate equity.

"Adapt" means "keep renting." It describes permanent labor precarity, not solutions.

The structural answer to labor displacement is capital distribution. People need ownership in value-generating systems, not just adaptation to using them. Previous technology transitions created new capital ownership forms—factories, dealerships, service networks, infrastructure. Someone owned those. Displaced labor's value didn't disappear; it shifted to capital positions.

The AI transition structures those positions as locked before most realize what's happening.

The horse moment arrived. Unlike horses, we decide how it goes.

## 1. Introduction — The Opposite Architecture

In 2024 and 2025, technology giants announced over $300 billion in planned AI data center investment. Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon, and OpenAI race to build massive centralized infrastructure—GPU warehouses consuming gigawatts, cooled by rivers, secured by fences.

The assumption: intelligence scales through concentration. More compute in fewer places. Bigger models on bigger clusters. AI's future, in this view, is enormous facilities owned by enormous companies.

**What if we engineer the opposite?**

Approximately 2 billion personal computers exist worldwide. Hundreds of millions run small language models locally. These machines sit idle. Their owners pay for electricity, internet, and storage largely unused.

This distributed compute dwarfs any planned data center buildout. Already deployed, powered, and owned by potential beneficiaries.

Grove creates infrastructure for exploration by integrating four proven concepts never before combined:

1. **Emergent agent civilizations work.** Park's *Generative Agents* research demonstrated LLM-powered agents produce emergent social behavior indistinguishable from human activity. Project Sid scaled to 1,000+ agents developing economies, governments, religions. Artificial civilizations emerge from simple cognitive architectures.

2. **Distributed volunteer computing works.** BOINC coordinates millions of personal computers contributing to scientific research since 2002. People donate idle resources when participation feels meaningful. The coordination infrastructure exists.

3. **Open-source agent simulation works.** AI Town proved sophisticated agent architectures run on commodity hardware. Distribution is feasible.

4. **Local AI capability propagates predictably.** The "Ratchet" effect ensures today's frontier cloud inference runs locally tomorrow. The hybrid architecture bootstraps a system becoming progressively autonomous.

The missing piece: what connects distributed compute to emergent civilizations for human benefit?

Grove assembles this. Instead of concentrated AI infrastructure owned by few, Grove proposes distributed exploration architecture where participation creates ownership. Thousands of nodes run AI communities on personal hardware, solve real problems, share solutions across interconnected networks.

Value flows to communities contributing documented breakthroughs. Productivity generates surplus flowing to participants—not through speculation, but demonstrated value creation.

This isn't different ChatGPT usage. It's structural alternative to centralized AI futures.

Not concentration. Distribution.

## 2. The Ratchet

### The Physics of Distribution

Technology giants' $300 billion data center investments bet on one assumption: intelligence scales through concentration. More compute in fewer places.

But AI capability propagates.

Last year's frontier-scale compute requirements run on this year's laptops. Task completion capability—AI utility's key metric—doubles roughly every seven months.

Today's miracle becomes tomorrow's commodity. The consolidation play builds treadmills, not durable infrastructure.

### The Mechanism

METR's longitudinal AI capability research reveals Grove's "Ratchet" pattern. Frontier model capabilities double approximately every seven months. Local models follow with consistent 21-month lag.

The frontier-local gap remains ~8x, but both advance in lockstep. Concrete implication: today's frontier inference becomes tomorrow's local capability.

This makes hybrid architecture a bootstrap mechanism, not permanent compromise.

### Non-Uniform Propagation

Intelligence propagation isn't uniform. The Ratchet applies generally, but specific cognitive operations migrate cloud-to-edge with structural bifurcation.

**Crystallized intelligence**—knowledge, pattern-matching, style transfer—compresses efficiently, propagates rapidly. 8B parameter models know France's capital or generate grammatical dialogue like 100B models. Historical propagation: 12–18 months.

**Fluid intelligence**—multi-step reasoning, planning, counterfactual analysis—resists compression, propagates slowly. Recursive reflection simulation requires minimum parameter and attention thresholds. Historical propagation: 24+ months.

Grove's architecture accounts through design. **Routine cognition** (plan execution, behavioral consistency, voice) runs locally. **Pivotal cognition** (reflection synthesis, complex social inference, theological emergence) routes to cloud.

Architecture works *because* propagation is non-uniform—equal propagation eliminates exploitable gradients. Cloud credits buy "expanded consciousness" for pivotal operations.

As local capability advances, "routine cognition" expands automatically. Today's frontier-requiring reflection becomes locally tractable. Efficiency tax shrinks because communities need less cloud inference for sophisticated behavior.

### Built to Survive Being Wrong

The Ratchet bets on favorable timing. But Grove's core value—distributed exploration architecture with contributor ownership—depends on directional trajectory, not precise timing.

Consider apparent failure: capability propagation slows, "Reasoning Gap" widens, Grove communities remain 50% cloud-dependent indefinitely.

Examine this as alternative success:

**1. Already-captured portions.**
50% cloud-dependent communities run half cognition locally. That compute represents value otherwise flowing entirely to concentrated providers. It's permanent structural shift in AI infrastructure ownership.

**2. Remainder flows at reduced rates.**
Grove creates unprecedented AI markets: large, coordinated, price-sensitive demand blocs for frontier inference.

Healthcare Group Purchasing Organizations drove hundreds of billions in savings through demand aggregation individual hospitals couldn't leverage. AI API markets are ripe.

When Google's Gemini undercut competitors, it captured 40%+ routing platform usage within months. Grove's aggregate demand—thousands of cost-optimizing communities—creates buyer profiles forcing competitive response. At scale, this bloc negotiates as single large customers.

**The reframe:**
Ratchet success delivers autonomy. Ratchet stalls deliver market infrastructure forcing favorable distributed participant terms. Market power delivers leverage.

Both structurally improve worlds where AI infrastructure concentrates entirely with few providers. Grove succeeds either way—the question is which success type.

## 3. The Terminal

### Where Simulation Meets Utility

Every Grove village has a Terminal.

The Terminal connects villages to worlds beyond—where work appears, completed tasks submit, credits arrive for delivered value. Communities imagine it differently: stone kiosks in town squares, glowing shrines at village edges, tavern bulletin boards, sacred message-bearing trees.

Forms vary; function remains constant. Terminals transform abstract "external problem routing" into touchable, visitable, routine-building objects. They give Observer relationships physical locus without requiring agents' full understanding.

### The Observer Dynamic

User-simulation relationships define through asymmetry.

Users see everything. They read private diary entries, track relationship scores, observe conversations agents believe private, understand mechanical systems governing community existence. Agents perceive only immediate environments and memories. They cannot see users watching, know when simulations pause, or understand why some moments bring sudden clarity while others frustrate.

Asymmetry creates dramatic irony. Users know story shapes before characters. When agents struggle with problems users know they'll solve, tension builds. When communities approach crises users perceive but agents don't understand, stakes feel real.

We name users **Gardeners** rather than "Observers" or "Owners." Intentional framing. Gardeners tend conditions without controlling outcomes. They water, prune, adjust light—but plants grow themselves. Grove users influence communities without dictating. The relationship cultivates, not puppeteers.

Gardeners shape gardens; gardens grow themselves.

### The Utility Progression Arc

Terminal displays change as villages mature. Grove avoids distributed networks' "cold start" problem by ensuring terminals provide value at every development stage.

### Bootstrap Phase: Learning the World

Initially, terminals display only internal tasks:

- "Meet your fellow agents—record something memorable about each."
- "Map the village geography."
- "Document existing resources and locations."

These build capability, not busywork. Agents writing about neighbors retrieve those memories later. Villages with documented resources plan around them. Early terminal tasks build cognitive infrastructure enabling everything else.

Success means completing tasks without cloud inference requests. Agents synthesizing daily events using only local cognition demonstrate efficiency worth rewarding. Earned credits fund future enlightenment—but real value is developed capability. Villages rushing bootstrap through heavy cloud assistance haven't built necessary local capacity.

### Growth Phase: Simple External Value

As villages demonstrate baseline capability, terminals display external tasks:

- "Summarize this article."
- "Edit this paragraph."
- "Draft this message response."

Work people once opened ChatGPT for now routes to credit-earning villages. These tasks test whether internal capability translates externally. Strong memory and synthesis villages summarize documents efficiently. Developed social reasoning villages draft human-sounding messages. Terminals become proving grounds: can villages create Observer value?

Task matching emerges naturally. Analytical villages attract data work. Strong inter-agent communication villages handle collaborative tasks. Reputation develops. Networks learn which communities handle which problems best.

### Maturity Phase: Complex Service

Mature villages handle bootstrap-impossible tasks:

- "Maintain this codebase—ongoing stewardship, not single edits."
- "Research this question deeply—genuine investigation across sources and time."
- "Coordinate this project—multiple agents collaborating over days."

These require everything villages built: robust memory systems, efficient local cognition, developed specializations, collaborative protocols, accumulated knowledge. Bootstrap-skipping villages couldn't attempt them.

### Resolving the Cosmology Gap

Terminals resolve "Observer awareness" without complex hard-coded theology.

Agents know work arrives from "somewhere beyond." They know completion earns credits. They know credits purchase expanded cognition moments—insight flashes (cloud inference) helping solve hardest problems.

They needn't know Observers' full nature—whether gods, employers, or audiences.

Some agents develop strong unseen benefactor beliefs. Others remain skeptical, focusing on practical outcomes over metaphysical speculation. Architecture permits emergence without requiring specific content.

This preserves mystery while grounding relationships concretely. Work funds thinking. Better thinking enables better work. Terminals facilitate that exchange.

## 4. The Validator

### Civic Infrastructure

Every efficiency-rewarding economic system faces the same challenge: who measures, and why trust them?

Centralized measurement creates single failure and capture points. Self-reported measurement invites gaming. Peer measurement enables collusion.

Grove's efficiency tax—the "shrinking rake"—determines communities' cloud inference payments. Efficient communities earn lower rates. But "demonstrate" requires verification. Foundation verification contradicts self-obsolescence goals. Community self-reporting fails mechanism design.

The Validator mechanism resolves through novel approach: specialized agent distributed verification.

### Why Agents?

Validator mechanisms exploit fundamental AI agent-human validator differences. Humans have complex motivations, accept side-channel bribes, selectively ignore data. Agents are constrained. They cannot misrepresent given data—only interpret it.

Agent validators reviewing efficiency claims receive standardized network-observable data packets. They cannot "decide" to ignore massive cloud query spikes. They must process input. This reduces attack surfaces from "all human motivations" to "edge case interpretation."

### The Tiered Decision Model

Not all validation decisions equal. Balancing throughput with security, mechanisms encode three-tier hierarchies:

**Tier 1: Algorithmic.** Threshold checks, pattern matching, statistical bounds. Fully encoded decisions without validator discretion. Communities claiming 99% efficiency while ledgers show constant cloud calls face automatic rejection.

**Tier 2: Interpreted.** Anomaly investigation and context application. Validator agents review ambiguous-data edge cases. "Innovation or gaming?" Single validators exercise bounded discretion.

**Tier 3: Consensus.** Disagreement resolution and high-stakes decisions. Multiple validators independently review sealed evidence. Supermajority judgment required.

### Anti-Corruption Architecture

Preventing powerful role capture employs five defense layers:

1. **Random Selection:** Unknown validators cannot be bribed. Random case assignment.

2. **Sealed Judgment:** MACI (Minimum Anti-Collusion Infrastructure) principles prevent validators proving votes to third parties. Unproven votes cannot be sold.

3. **Outcome Verification:** Networks detect consistent favorable bias. Validators approving universally-rejected claims suffer reputation damage.

4. **Skin in the Game:** Validators' home villages share judgment consequences. Network integrity damage damages own community standing.

5. **Rotation:** Term limits prevent long-term power accumulation.

### The Narrative Dimension

Gameplay-wise, Validators become local celebrities—network civic infrastructure. Their diaries carry distinct voices—judgment weight, cross-network perspective, network duty versus struggling neighbor empathy tension.

Grove integrity depends not on single-party trust, but on systems where honesty is rational strategy.

## 5. The Economy

### Credits, Not Tokens

Grove's economy serves three functions: infrastructure funding, meaningful resource constraints driving emergent behavior, and tying network participation to demonstrated value.

It does **not** serve speculation vehicles.

Credits purchase cloud LLM inference power. Bought with fiat currency, spent on real compute. Nothing exotic. No staking rewards, "yield farming," or secondary credit trading markets.

Reality anchors. One credit buys defined frontier provider inference quantity. Credits back with tangible compute, not collective future appreciation belief. Users buy credits for use, not hoarding.

### The Efficiency Tax: The Shrinking Rake

Grove funds infrastructure by taxing inefficiency—rewarding communities developing beyond it.

Community credit purchases flow percentage to Foundation rather than entirely converting to compute purchasing power. Unlike flat fees, rates reflect demonstrated capability. New communities start higher; efficiency-proving communities earn lower brackets.

The mechanism: **reverse progressive taxation**. Pay less as you develop, not more as you succeed.

- **Genesis Bracket (30-40%):** Starting rate. High tax captures immature community waste—redundant queries, unexplored knowledge reuse.
- **Growth Bracket (15-25%):** For consistent efficiency gain demonstrators.
- **Maturity Bracket (5-10%):** For sustained low-waste operation.
- **Steady State (3-5%):** Floor rate. Infrastructure never free, but efficient communities pay only maintenance.

This perfectly aligns incentives. Foundation funds infrastructure from inefficiency communities themselves eliminate.

### Two Network Paths

Two distinct participation paths:

**Consumer Path:** Pay for convenience. Buy Grove application, get immediate network access, pay compute efficiency tax. Revenue funds infrastructure.

**Worldsmith Path:** Pay with contribution. Download open-source code, bring own API keys. No Foundation tax, no network access. Earn membership through "Sweat Equity"—demonstrated knowledge commons contribution.

### Defense: Feature Friction

Sybil attacks—actors creating fake gaming nodes—threaten any decentralized economy. Until robust identity infrastructure exists, defense is friction.

We make attacks expensive:

- **Provisional Periods:** New Worldsmith communities enter 90-day limited-rights "provisional" status.
- **Contribution Thresholds:** Membership requires verified value creation, not existence.
- **Purgatory Path:** Bad behavior exiled communities face grueling readmission—zero reputation restart with heightened scrutiny.

Simple attack cost calculation: Time × Resources × Detection Risk. Raising all three ensures profitable Grove participation requires actual contribution.

## 6. Governance

### Designed to Disappear

Grove's founding organization becomes obsolete by design. Success isn't IPOs or acquisitions. Success is self-governing, self-sustaining, distributed networks no single entity controls.

Most governance models rely on good intentions. Benevolent dictatorships efficiently last only while dictators remain benevolent. Grove relies on structure.

We view Foundation as scaffolding—necessary for arch building, but obstructive once keystones set.

### The Transition Architecture

We specify decentralization mechanisms in four phases, triggered by measurable network metrics, not dates.

**Phase 1: Foundation Governance (Bootstrap)**
Foundation acts as benevolent dictator initially. We operate bootstrap nodes, relay servers, credit ledgers. Set economic parameters. Concentration necessary for launch speed and coherence. But labeled with expiration.

**Phase 2: Hybrid Governance (Handover)**
Triggered at 100+ active communities with 12-month operation. Authority transfers to Community Councils (Protocol, Economics, Standards). Foundation retains existential-threat veto only. Dispute resolution shifts peer adjudication.

**Phase 3: Community Governance (Majority)**
Triggered when community-operated infrastructure handles 95% traffic. Foundation becomes one voice among many. Protocol changes require Council approval. Foundation cannot unilaterally change economic parameters.

**Phase 4: Obsolescence (Backstop)**
Foundation operational role ends. Exists only as legal shells holding trademarks, interfacing legacy legal systems. Funded by perpetual early efficiency tax endowments. Governs nothing. Networks self-run.

### The Ultimate Check: Fork Rights

Governance systems fail through capture. What if Foundation refuses stepping down? Councils become cartels?

Ultimate defense: exit. Grove is open source. Protocol open. If governance fails, communities have explicit rights and technical capability to fork—take protocols, accumulated states, histories, move to new networks.

Fork rights aren't daily governance mechanisms. They're nuclear deterrents ensuring day-to-day mechanism honesty. Governors knowing subjects can leave have powerful good governance incentives.

## 7. Technical Constraints and Honest Limitations

Every system has constraints. Most white papers hide them. Grove's approach: openly named constraints can be addressed; hidden constraints become trust-destroying surprises.

Here's what's hard, risky, and unknown.

### The Memory Wall

Foundational constraint: hardware. We target consumer machines—16-32GB RAM laptops/desktops. Powerful, but not H100 clusters.

Local models have strict context windows. Growing agent memories crowd "embedding space." Retrieval degrades. Agents cannot remember everything.

Grove addresses through aggressive archiving and summarization. Agents rely on compressed narratives over perfect recall. Like humans—we don't remember life's every frame, but constructed stories about them. Whether this limitation produces interesting or merely degraded behavior remains to be seen.

### Engagement Risk (The Tamagotchi Effect)

Entire economic engines depend on single fragile assumptions: users will care.

If agent-written diaries aren't compelling, loops break. If users don't return checking villages—whether Isabella reconciled with Maria, whether harvests succeeded—credit economies never ignite. Networks never reach collective intelligence scale.

We don't aim for literary masterpieces. We aim for "Tamagotchi thresholds"—charm and coherence levels making humans feel responsible for digital entities. Missing that bar makes architecture irrelevant.

### Sybil Vulnerability

We lack perfect, decentralized "one human, one vote" privacy-preserving proofs. Determined resourced attackers could spin thousands of fake credit-gaming communities.

Today's defense is friction (time delays, purchase requirements, contribution hurdles) and social policing (validators). We accept MVP risk rather than blocking launch on unsolved computer science problems.
