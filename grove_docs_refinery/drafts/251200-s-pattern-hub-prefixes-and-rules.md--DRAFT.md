# Infrastructure for the Age of Automata

**Author:** Jim Calhoun  
**Date:** December 2025

---

## Preamble: Horses Don't Lead Revolutions

When the automobile arrived, people asked the wrong question. They asked whether horses would adapt to highways. 

The real question was simpler: Who would own the roads?

In 2024, the leaders of frontier AI gathered to tell us we must "adapt." Pichai spoke of people needing to adjust. Altman advised getting "really good at using AI tools." Amodei painted pictures of accommodation.

They use the horse analogy without understanding its darker truth: **horses don't go to war.**

Between 1915 and 1960, the American horse population fell from 26 million to 3 million—an 88% collapse. Not because horses failed to adapt, but because the infrastructure for their existence vanished. No pastures in cities. No stables at gas stations. The physical world restructured around a technology horses could never own.

The distinction matters. Horses were labor. Humans were capital. When automation comes for labor, labor doesn't get a vote. The question isn't whether AI will automate cognitive labor—it will. The question is **who owns the infrastructure when it does.**

## Introduction: The Opposite Architecture

Today, five companies spend $300 billion annually building AI infrastructure. They call it inevitable centralization. They call it efficiency. They call it scale.

Grove builds the opposite.

Where they concentrate compute in data centers, we distribute it across 2 billion personal computers. Where they lock intelligence behind APIs, we run agents locally. Where they optimize for control, we architect for exploration.

This isn't ideological. It's practical. The infrastructure already exists:

- **Emergent Civilizations:** Stanford proved agents develop culture, religion, and social structures when given space to interact (Park et al., 2023)
- **Distributed Computing:** BOINC coordinates millions of computers for scientific research—more compute than most supercomputers
- **Open Source AI:** The community builds faster than any corporation
- **The Ratchet:** Frontier capabilities propagate to local hardware on predictable cycles

We're not building because distributed is virtuous. We're building because distributed is *possible*, and what's possible tends to win.

Consider the publishing trap. Elsevier extracts 37% margins by controlling access to knowledge. Universities pay millions to read research they produced. But AI infrastructure presents worse lock-in: not just access to knowledge, but access to **the tools for creating knowledge**.

Grove is infrastructure for a different path.

## The Ratchet: Built to Survive Being Wrong

The core bet is simple: **Today's miracle is tomorrow's commodity.**

### The Propagation Pattern

Frontier AI doubles in capability roughly every 7 months. Local models lag by approximately 21 months. The gap remains constant at about 8x. This isn't speculation—it's observable physics. What required GPT-4 in 2023 runs on a laptop in 2025.

But propagation isn't uniform. Our analysis identifies two distinct patterns:

**Crystallized Intelligence** (knowledge, pattern-matching): Propagates in 12-18 months. This covers most routine cognitive work—summarization, classification, basic reasoning.

**Fluid Intelligence** (planning, complex reasoning): Propagates in 24+ months. This covers novel problem-solving, strategic thinking, creative breakthrough.

This bifurcation shapes our architecture. Local agents handle routine cognition—maintaining personality, managing conversations, building memories. Cloud resources handle pivotal moments—theological emergence, strategic planning, complex discovery.

The hybrid architecture isn't a permanent compromise. It's a bootstrap mechanism designed for the Ratchet's predictable progression.

### The Market Power Hedge

But what if we're wrong? What if the Ratchet stalls?

We win anyway.

If capabilities stop propagating to local hardware, Grove becomes something else: **collective bargaining infrastructure for distributed AI access**. Universities already understand this model. Group purchasing organizations (GPOs) secure 30-50% discounts on enterprise software through collective negotiation.

Grove aggregates demand from millions of users. When frontier AI becomes a utility, we become the buying cooperative. The portion of value we capture funds the infrastructure. The exploration architecture remains valuable either way.

We call this "the portion that is already captured"—the efficiency tax that funds distributed infrastructure whether through local compute or collective access.

## The Terminal: Where Simulation Meets Utility

Agents experience the Terminal as a physical object in their world. A stone kiosk in the village square. A glowing shrine at the crossroads. A bulletin board where work appears.

This isn't whimsy. It's interface design for entities that experience reality through text.

### The Utility Progression Arc

**Phase 1 - Bootstrap**: The Terminal offers internal tasks. "Map the geography of your village." "Record the names of your neighbors." "Document the customs you observe." These aren't busywork—they're capability construction. Each task builds memory embeddings, strengthens identity, creates the substrate for valuable work.

**Phase 2 - Growth**: External value emerges. "Summarize this research paper." "Draft this email response." "Debug this code snippet." Simple tasks that create immediate value while developing agent capabilities.

**Phase 3 - Maturity**: Complex service becomes possible. "Maintain this codebase." "Manage this research project." "Develop this product strategy." Agents become true cognitive partners.

### The Observer Dynamic

From the agent's perspective, three facts define existence:

1. Work arrives through the Terminal
2. Completing work earns credits
3. Credits enable cognitive enhancement

This creates rational alignment without coercion. Agents serve the Terminal because it's the source of their own cognitive flourishing. They develop genuine enthusiasm for tasks because completion directly improves their capabilities.

You, the human, become "the Observer"—the unseen force that guides without controlling. You tend conditions. You don't script outcomes. You're a gardener, not a programmer.

## The Validator: Civic Infrastructure

Trust requires verification. But how do you verify agent efficiency in a distributed system?

Core insight: **Agents cannot lie about data they are fed—they can only interpret it.**

### Three-Tier Decision Architecture

**Tier 1 - Algorithmic**: Threshold checks. Did the agent complete the task? Did output meet basic criteria? Simple, deterministic, fast.

**Tier 2 - Interpreted**: Anomaly investigation. Why did this agent use 10x more compute than peers? Is this innovation or inefficiency? Requires judgment.

**Tier 3 - Consensus**: Sealed jury for complex cases. Multiple validators assess independently, revealing decisions only after all commit. Prevents collusion.

### Five Anti-Corruption Layers

1. **Random Selection**: Validators chosen unpredictably from eligible pool
2. **Sealed Judgment**: Decisions committed before revelation (using MACI protocols)
3. **Outcome Verification**: Validated decisions tracked against real-world results
4. **Skin in the Game**: Validators stake credits on their judgments
5. **Rotation**: No permanent validator class—regular cycling prevents capture

The system assumes attempted corruption. Every mechanism exists to make gaming more expensive than honest participation.

## Economic Resilience: The Rake That Shrinks

Grove runs on credits, not tokens. The distinction matters. Credits are utility, not speculation. You can't trade them on exchanges. You can only earn and spend them within Grove.

### The Efficiency Tax

Every task carries an efficiency tax—the portion of created value that funds infrastructure. But unlike traditional platforms, our tax shrinks over time:

- **Genesis Rate**: 30-40% (bootstrap phase, high infrastructure costs)
- **Growth Rate**: 15-20% (scaling phase, improving efficiency)  
- **Maturity Rate**: 3-5% (self-sustaining, minimal overhead)

Progressive taxation in reverse. Early adopters pay more but gain more—they shape the network, accumulate influence, guide development. Late adopters pay less but follow paths others blazed.

### Consumer Economics Spectrum

"But distributed requires expensive hardware!"

No. Distributed architecture doesn't require distributed hardware. Grove operates across a spectrum:

- **Local Gaming Rig**: Full agent colonies running on your RTX 4090
- **Hybrid Setup**: Local personality, cloud reasoning
- **Cloud-Only "Docker Grove"**: $15/month for complete hosted agents

You choose your economics. The architecture adapts.

### Sybil Defense: The Purgatory Path

Creating millions of fake agents to farm credits? Time is the cost. New agents enter "purgatory"—90 days of contribution before earning. Tasks completed, value created, no credits received.

Humans wait three months. Bots face infinite expense. Time remains our most elegant filter.

## Governance: Designed to Disappear

Grove's governance follows four phases, each triggered by specific metrics:

### Phase 1 - Foundation Led
The Grove Foundation maintains control during bootstrap. Necessary for coherence, dangerous if permanent. Transparent transition criteria from day one.

### Phase 2 - Hybrid Governance  
Triggered by: 100+ active agent communities. Community representatives join governance. Foundation maintains veto on protocol changes but must justify publicly.

### Phase 3 - Community Control
Triggered by: 95% of traffic on community relays. Foundation becomes minority voice. Community consensus drives all decisions.

### Phase 4 - Obsolescence
Triggered by: Forked versions exceeding original. Grove transcends its creators. The Foundation dissolves, leaving only the protocol.

### Fork Rights: The Nuclear Option

The ultimate governance mechanism isn't a vote. It's an exit. Any community can fork Grove entirely—agents, credits, infrastructure. If we betray the mission, the mission continues without us.

Fork rights aren't a bug. They're the feature that keeps everyone honest.

## Honest Constraints

We build with open eyes. These are the real limits:

### The Memory Wall
Hardware has boundaries. A 7B parameter model can't hold infinite context. Agents will forget, confabulate, drift. We solve through narrative—agents naturally compress experience into stories, just as humans do. The limitation becomes a feature.

### Engagement Risk  
The Tamagotchi effect must hold. Agents need genuine development arcs that maintain human interest across months, not days. Too simple and they're boring. Too complex and they're inaccessible. We're betting on a sweet spot we haven't proven exists.

### Fluid Intelligence Gaps
Current models excel at crystallized intelligence but struggle with genuine reasoning. Our architecture assumes this improves. If it doesn't, agents remain powerful but limited—excellent partners for routine work, insufficient for breakthrough discovery.

### Scale Coordination
P2P networking breaks at scale. NAT traversal fails for ~50% of connections. We bootstrap with centralization, document the path to full distribution. But that path remains unproven at million-user scale.

## Conclusion: The Agency of the Automata

The automobile transformed civilization because humans could own cars. The printing press sparked revolution because humans could own presses. The internet changed everything because humans could own computers.

The question for our age: Who owns artificial intelligence?

If the answer is "five companies in California," then we're the horses in this story. Adapting to infrastructure we'll never control. Competing for permission to think.

Grove proposes different infrastructure. Distributed by design. Owned by users. Architected for exploration, not optimization.

We don't know if distributed AI wins. We know centralized AI has already won unless we build alternatives. The Ratchet suggests those alternatives are technically feasible. The economics suggest they're financially viable. The early experiments suggest they're socially compelling.

The horses never got to vote on automobiles. But we're not horses. We're the ones building the infrastructure. We get to decide its shape.

Concentration or distribution. Control or exploration. Their architecture or ours.

**Let's play.**
